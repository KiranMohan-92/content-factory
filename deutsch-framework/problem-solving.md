# Problem-Solving: Deutsch's Framework

## The Two Fundamental Principles

### 1. Problems Are Inevitable
**Meaning**: You cannot avoid problems. Every solution creates new problems.

**Why**:
- Solving a problem changes the situation
- New situations reveal new problems
- This is not a bug—it's the nature of progress
- Attempting to avoid problems is itself a problem

**Example**:
- Cure a disease → population grows → need more food
- Invent computers → creates cybersecurity problems
- Achieve wealth → creates problems of meaning and purpose
- Each solved problem opens new vistas with new challenges

### 2. Problems Are Soluble
**Meaning**: All problems that don't violate the laws of physics are solvable in principle.

**Why**:
- Knowledge can grow without bound
- Humans are universal explainers
- No fundamentally insoluble problems exist
- Only insufficient knowledge

**Crucial Caveat**:
- Not "easily" soluble
- Not "quickly" soluble
- Not "cheaply" soluble
- But: Given enough knowledge creation, soluble

## What Is a Problem?

### Deutsch's Definition
A problem is a conflict between ideas (theories, preferences, interpretations, goals).

**Not**:
- Just an obstacle
- Just a difficulty
- Just dissatisfaction

**But**:
- A conflict in our understanding
- Competing explanations that can't all be true
- Contradictions that demand resolution

### Types of Problems

#### 1. Scientific Problems
**Conflict between**: Theory and observation, or between theories

**Example**:
- Mercury's orbit conflicted with Newtonian gravity
- Created a problem
- Solved by General Relativity

#### 2. Practical Problems
**Conflict between**: Current situation and desired situation

**Example**:
- Want to travel faster, but current tech limits us
- Conflict between goal and reality
- Solved by better engines, materials, designs

#### 3. Philosophical Problems
**Conflict between**: Different interpretations or frameworks

**Example**:
- Free will vs. determinism
- Conflict between intuitions
- Requires better understanding of causation, computation, choice

#### 4. Moral Problems
**Conflict between**: Different values or their applications

**Example**:
- Freedom vs. security
- Individual rights vs. collective good
- Requires better moral explanations

## The Problem-Solving Process

### Step 1: Recognize the Problem
**Key Point**: Problems aren't always obvious.

**Tasks**:
- Identify the conflict
- Articulate what's contradictory
- Don't dismiss the problem as "just how things are"
- Surface hidden assumptions

### Step 2: Conjecture Solutions
**Key Point**: Solutions come from creativity, not procedure.

**Tasks**:
- Propose explanations that resolve the conflict
- Don't wait for "enough information"
- Generate multiple competing conjectures
- Be bold—timid solutions rarely work

### Step 3: Criticize Conjectures
**Key Point**: Rational criticism, not just testing.

**Tasks**:
- Look for internal contradictions
- Check against other good explanations
- Identify what would refute each conjecture
- Use thought experiments
- Eventually: empirical tests

### Step 4: Eliminate Errors
**Key Point**: Reject failed solutions, learn from them.

**Tasks**:
- Abandon refuted conjectures decisively
- Understand why they failed
- Extract insights for next conjecture
- Don't cling to failed ideas

### Step 5: Create New Problems
**Key Point**: Each solution opens new questions.

**Tasks**:
- Identify problems created by the solution
- Recognize new opportunities
- Frame the next problem
- Continue the cycle

## Rational Optimism

### What It Is
**Definition**: The principle that all evils are due to lack of knowledge.

**Corollary**: All problems are soluble (with the right knowledge).

### What It Isn't
- Not: "Everything will automatically be fine"
- Not: "Problems don't matter"
- Not: "Ignore difficulties"
- Not: Blind faith or wishful thinking

### What It Is
- All problems stem from insufficient knowledge
- More knowledge can solve those problems
- Knowledge growth is unbounded
- Therefore: rational basis for optimism

### The Argument for Optimism

**Premise 1**: Problems are conflicts requiring better explanations
**Premise 2**: Knowledge (explanations) can grow without limit
**Premise 3**: Humans are universal explainers (can create any knowledge in principle)
**Conclusion**: Any problem is soluble in principle with sufficient knowledge

### Pessimism as Failure of Imagination
**Pessimism claims**: Some problems are fundamentally insoluble.

**Deutsch's response**:
- This is always an argument from ignorance
- "We can't see how to solve it" ≠ "It's insoluble"
- Historically, "impossible" problems get solved
- Pessimism prevents us from looking for solutions

**Examples of Failed Pessimism**:
- "Heavier-than-air flight is impossible"
- "Space travel will never be practical"
- "We'll never understand the atom"
- "Communication faster than horses is impossible"

## Common Problem-Solving Errors

### Error 1: Seeking the "Final Solution"
**Mistake**: Trying to solve all future problems at once.

**Reality**:
- Each solution creates new problems
- This is good—new problems mean progress
- Embrace the infinite cycle
- Don't seek stasis

### Error 2: Prophesying Problems
**Mistake**: Predicting what problems will occur, then trying to prevent them.

**Reality**:
- Future problems are unpredictable (involve new knowledge)
- Can't solve problems you don't understand yet
- Better to build knowledge-creation capacity
- Flexibility beats prediction

### Error 3: Precautionary Principle
**Mistake**: "Avoid anything with unknown risks."

**Reality**:
- Everything has unknown risks
- Inaction has unknown risks too
- Progress requires taking risks
- Better principle: maximize error-correction capacity

### Error 4: Waiting for Certainty
**Mistake**: "We need more data before acting."

**Reality**:
- You'll never have certainty
- Knowledge grows through conjecture and error
- Act on best current explanations
- Be ready to correct errors

### Error 5: Authority Worship
**Mistake**: "Experts say it's impossible."

**Reality**:
- No authority can judge what's possible
- "Impossible" often means "we don't know how yet"
- Expertise is valuable but fallible
- Good explanations trump authority

### Error 6: Treating Symptoms
**Mistake**: Addressing surface issues, not underlying conflicts.

**Reality**:
- Find the actual conflict
- Surface problems often point to deeper issues
- Solve the root explanatory conflict
- Superficial fixes create new problems

## Error Correction: The Key to Progress

### Why Error Correction Matters More Than Perfection

**Key Insight**: Good problem-solving isn't about avoiding errors—it's about correcting them rapidly.

**Implications**:
- Design for error detection
- Build in correction mechanisms
- Embrace criticism
- Iterate quickly

### Systems That Support Error Correction

#### 1. Scientific Method
- Publish theories openly
- Invite criticism
- Replicate experiments
- Reject refuted theories

#### 2. Liberal Democracy
- Free speech allows criticism
- Elections allow error correction
- Peaceful removal of failed policies
- Multiple parties = multiple conjectures

#### 3. Market Economy
- Profit/loss reveals errors
- Bankruptcy eliminates bad ideas
- Prices communicate information
- Competition generates alternatives

#### 4. Legal System (at its best)
- Adversarial process
- Appeals process
- Precedent revision
- Explicit error correction

### Building Error Correction Into Solutions

**When solving problems, ask**:
1. How will we know if this is wrong?
2. How can we correct it if it fails?
3. What's the cost of reversal?
4. Are we making this hard to change?
5. Can we test small-scale first?

## Universality and Problem-Solving

### Universal Explainers
**Definition**: Systems that can create any knowledge, in principle.

**Example**: Human beings (possibly AI in future).

**Implication**:
- Humans can understand anything that can be understood
- No problem is beyond human comprehension in principle
- We might lack the knowledge now, but can create it

### Universal Constructors
**Definition**: Systems that can build anything that can be built.

**Implication**:
- If something is physically possible, we can build it
- Limitations are always knowledge limitations
- Technology progress is unbounded

### What Universality Means for Problems
- No inherent ceiling on what we can solve
- Current inability doesn't imply fundamental impossibility
- Rational to expect solutions to emerge

## Practical Problem-Solving Heuristics

### 1. Start With the Problem, Not the Solution
- Understand the actual conflict
- Don't jump to familiar solutions
- Question whether it's really the problem

### 2. Look for Good Explanations
- What would explain this situation?
- What mechanism is at work?
- Hard-to-vary explanations are better

### 3. Generate Multiple Conjectures
- Don't settle for first idea
- Competing conjectures reveal issues
- Criticism needs alternatives

### 4. Seek Criticism Actively
- Find the strongest objections
- Steel-man opposing views
- Your goal is truth, not winning

### 5. Make Conjectures Testable
- What would show this is wrong?
- Design experiments/tests
- Be specific about predictions

### 6. Fail Fast, Learn Fast
- Test small and early
- Don't invest heavily before testing
- Each failure teaches something

### 7. Build on Good Explanations
- Connect to existing knowledge
- Look for reach
- Integrate solutions

### 8. Accept New Problems
- Don't be discouraged by them
- They signal progress
- Frame them constructively

## Case Study Template: Analyzing Problems

### Problem Structure
1. **What is the conflict?** (Be specific)
2. **Why does this conflict exist?** (Root cause)
3. **What bad explanations exist?** (Common but wrong answers)
4. **What good explanations might work?** (Hard-to-vary conjectures)

### Solution Evaluation
5. **Is this explanation hard to vary?** (Test it)
6. **What does it predict?** (Including unexpected things)
7. **How could it be wrong?** (Rejectability)
8. **What new problems does it create?** (Inevitable consequence)

### Implementation
9. **How can we test this?** (Small scale first)
10. **How can we detect errors?** (Monitoring)
11. **How can we correct errors?** (Reversibility)
12. **What's the next iteration?** (Continuous improvement)

## The Infinite Regress of Problem-Solving

### The Pattern
Problem₁ → Solution₁ → Problem₂ → Solution₂ → Problem₃ → ...

**This is not a bug. This IS progress.**

### Why This Is Good
- Each cycle increases knowledge
- New problems are "better" problems (require more knowledge)
- Indicates growth, not failure
- Enables open-ended improvement

### The Beginning of Infinity
- No final problem
- No end to progress
- Always more to understand
- Always new frontiers

**This is cause for optimism, not despair.**

## Key Principles Summary

1. **Problems are inevitable** - Accept and embrace this
2. **Problems are soluble** - With sufficient knowledge
3. **Knowledge is unbounded** - No fundamental limits
4. **Optimism is rational** - Based on these principles
5. **Error correction beats perfection** - Design for it
6. **Universality removes ceilings** - Humans can solve anything in principle
7. **New problems signal progress** - Not failure
8. **Good explanations drive solutions** - Mechanism matters

## Questions for Applying This Framework

When facing any problem:
1. What is the actual conflict here?
2. What knowledge would solve this?
3. What bad explanations are blocking progress?
4. What good explanations might work?
5. How can I test my conjectures?
6. How can I design for error correction?
7. What new problems will success create?
8. Am I being pessimistic without good reason?
