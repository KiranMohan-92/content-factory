# Good Explanations: The Heart of Knowledge

## What Is a Good Explanation?

**Deutsch's Definition**: A good explanation is hard to vary while still accounting for what it purports to account for.

This simple criterion is revolutionary. It distinguishes real knowledge from pseudo-knowledge, science from myth, and progress from stagnation.

## The Hard-to-Vary Criterion

### Core Concept
An explanation is good when you can't change its details without destroying its ability to explain.

**Example - Bad Explanation**:
*"Seasons happen because the gods want variety in weather."*
- You could swap "gods" for "nature spirits" or "cosmic forces"
- You could change "variety" to "testing humans" or "aesthetic beauty"
- All variations "explain" seasons equally well
- Therefore, it's a bad explanation (easy to vary)

**Example - Good Explanation**:
*"Seasons happen because Earth's axis is tilted 23.5° relative to its orbital plane, causing different hemispheres to receive varying amounts of direct sunlight as Earth orbits the Sun."*
- Change the tilt angle arbitrarily → doesn't explain the specific season pattern
- Remove the tilt → doesn't explain seasons at all
- Change orbital mechanics → contradicts observations
- Hard to vary while keeping explanatory power
- Therefore, it's a good explanation

### Why This Works
- Hard-to-vary explanations are constrained by reality
- Easy-to-vary explanations are unconstrained (can explain anything)
- If an explanation can explain anything, it explains nothing
- Constraint = contact with reality = good explanation

## Reach: The Second Criterion

### What Is Reach?
**Reach**: The ability of an explanation to explain more than it was originally designed to explain.

### Examples of Reach

**Newton's Gravity**:
- Designed to explain: Planetary orbits
- Also explained: Tides, falling objects, projectile motion, pendulums
- Even explained: Things not yet observed (Neptune's existence)
- **High reach**

**Quantum Theory**:
- Designed to explain: Atomic spectra
- Also explained: Chemical bonding, solid-state physics, superconductivity, lasers
- Now explains: Computation, cryptography, biological processes
- **Extraordinary reach**

**Astrology**:
- Designed to explain: Human personality and events
- Also explains: Nothing else
- Must be modified for each new case
- **No reach**

### Why Reach Matters
- Good explanations have deep connections to reality
- These connections extend beyond the original domain
- Reach is a sign of fundamental truth
- Parochial explanations rarely have reach

## Distinguishing Good from Bad Explanations

### The Variation Test
**Ask**: "Can I change the details while keeping the explanatory power?"

If YES → Bad explanation (easy to vary)
If NO → Good explanation (hard to vary)

### Common Bad Explanation Patterns

#### 1. Appeals to Authority
*"It works because the expert/ancient wisdom/holy text says so."*
- Swap the authority → explanation still "works"
- Easy to vary
- No actual explanation of mechanism

#### 2. Just-So Stories
*"Zebras have stripes to confuse predators."*
- Could be "to recognize each other"
- Could be "to regulate temperature"
- Could be "because ancestral environment"
- Easy to vary (though some variants are better than others)
- Needs mechanism to be good explanation

#### 3. Anthropic Reasoning
*"The universe is fine-tuned for life because otherwise we wouldn't be here to observe it."*
- Doesn't explain *why* these constants
- Doesn't explain the mechanism
- Easy to vary the supporting details
- Circular reasoning

#### 4. Epicycles (Ad Hoc Modifications)
*Adding arbitrary adjustments to save a theory*
- Each adjustment can be varied
- Core theory becomes unfalsifiable
- Easy to vary in aggregate

### Common Good Explanation Patterns

#### 1. Mechanistic Explanations
*Explain HOW something works*
- Evolution by natural selection (mechanism of variation + selection)
- Germ theory (mechanism of infection)
- Market economics (mechanism of price discovery)

#### 2. Fundamental Principles
*Connect to deep laws*
- Conservation laws
- Symmetry principles
- Computational universality

#### 3. Mathematical Structure
*Precise quantitative relationships*
- F = ma
- E = mc²
- Schrödinger equation

## Rejectability: The Test of Goodness

### Good Explanations Are Risky
**Key Insight**: The harder an explanation is to vary, the easier it is to reject.

**Why**:
- Hard-to-vary = specific predictions
- Specific predictions = many ways to be wrong
- Many ways to be wrong = highly testable
- Highly testable = risky but powerful

**Example**:
- "Earth's tilt is 23.5°" is risky (measure the tilt → could be wrong)
- "Gods want variety" is safe (can't measure divine preferences)
- The risky explanation is better because it can be tested

### The Paradox of Good Explanations
- They're hard to vary (seem rigid)
- But easy to reject (seem fragile)
- This is a feature, not a bug
- Bad explanations are the opposite: easy to vary, hard to reject

## Practical Application: Evaluating Explanations

### The Good Explanation Checklist

**1. Hard-to-Vary Test**
- Can I change details while keeping explanatory power?
- Does it make specific, necessary claims?

**2. Reach Test**
- Does it explain more than originally intended?
- Do predictions extend beyond the domain?

**3. Rejectability Test**
- What observation would refute this?
- Is it making risky predictions?

**4. Mechanism Test**
- Does it explain *how*, not just *what*?
- Is there a coherent causal story?

**5. Integration Test**
- Does it connect to other good explanations?
- Or is it isolated and parochial?

## Common Confusions

### "Simplicity = Good Explanation"
**Not Quite**: Occam's Razor is often misunderstood.

**Wrong Version**: "Simpler theories are more likely to be true"
**Right Version**: "Don't add unnecessary components to explanations"

**Deutsch's View**:
- Simplicity is about hard-to-vary, not minimal components
- A complex but hard-to-vary explanation beats a simple but easy-to-vary one
- Quantum theory is complex but good
- "God did it" is simple but bad

### "Empirical Support = Good Explanation"
**Wrong**: Explanations can have empirical support but still be bad.

**Example**:
- Ancient astronomy had excellent predictions (for limited domain)
- But explanations involved epicycles and special celestial properties
- Easy to vary → bad explanations
- Despite good empirical track record

**Right Approach**: Evaluate the explanation itself, not just predictions.

### "Falsifiability = Good Explanation"
**Incomplete**: Popper's criterion is necessary but not sufficient.

**Example**:
- "Tomorrow's lottery numbers will be 7, 14, 21, 28, 35, 42"
- Highly falsifiable
- But terrible explanation (arbitrary)

**Deutsch's Addition**: Must be hard-to-vary AND falsifiable.

## Examples Across Domains

### Physics
**Bad**: "Atoms exist because that's how nature built things"
**Good**: Atomic theory with specific structure, explaining chemistry, thermodynamics, spectroscopy

### Biology
**Bad**: "Species are designed for their environments"
**Good**: Evolution by natural selection (variation + selection + inheritance)

### Economics
**Bad**: "Prices are set by greedy capitalists"
**Good**: Price mechanism as distributed information processor (Hayek)

### Medicine
**Bad**: "This herb cures illness because of ancient wisdom"
**Good**: Germ theory identifying specific pathogens and mechanisms

### Software
**Bad**: "It works because I followed best practices"
**Good**: Understanding the computational model and why specific algorithms are correct

## Creating Good Explanations

### The Process
1. **Identify the phenomenon** to explain
2. **Propose a mechanism** (conjecture)
3. **Make it specific** (hard to vary)
4. **Derive predictions** (including unexpected ones = reach)
5. **Test against criticism** (including experiments)
6. **Refine or reject** based on errors found

### Warning Signs of Bad Explanations
- Relies on "it's obvious" or "everyone knows"
- Appeals to authority or tradition
- Can be modified to fit any observation
- Has no specific mechanism
- Makes no risky predictions
- Isolated from other knowledge
- Shuts down further questions

### Signs of Good Explanations
- Makes you go "Aha! That's why!"
- Connects previously disconnected phenomena
- Makes unexpected predictions
- Has specific, necessary components
- Invites further questions
- Integrates with other good explanations
- Could easily be wrong (but isn't)

## The Explanatory Power of Good Explanations

### Why Good Explanations Matter for Progress

**1. Enable Further Discovery**
- Good explanations point to new questions
- Create new problems to solve
- Open new domains of inquiry

**2. Survive Criticism**
- Stand up to rational argument
- Withstand experimental test
- Get refined, not abandoned

**3. Unify Knowledge**
- Connect different domains
- Reveal deep patterns
- Build coherent worldview

**4. Guide Action**
- Reliable basis for decisions
- Predict consequences accurately
- Enable technology

## Key Takeaways

1. **Good explanations are hard to vary** while still explaining what they purport to explain
2. **Reach is a sign of deep truth** - explaining more than intended
3. **Good explanations are risky** - easy to test and reject
4. **Mechanism matters more than prediction** - understanding beats forecasting
5. **Evaluation is objective** - some explanations are genuinely better than others
6. **Progress comes from better explanations** - not just more data

## Questions to Ask

When evaluating any explanation:
1. What is this actually explaining?
2. Can I vary the details while keeping it explanatory?
3. What else does this explain beyond its original domain?
4. What would show this explanation is wrong?
5. Does it tell me *how* or just *what*?
6. How does it connect to other good explanations?
7. Does it close off questions or open new ones?
