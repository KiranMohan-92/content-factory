# YouTube Script Template

## Video Length
- **Short-form** (5-8 minutes): 750-1200 words
- **Medium-form** (10-15 minutes): 1500-2250 words
- **Long-form** (20-30 minutes): 3000-4500 words

## Script Format

All scripts use this notation:
```
[VISUAL: Description of what appears on screen]
NARRATION: What you say out loud
[ACTION: What you do - gesture, pause, etc.]
```

---

## STRUCTURE

### COLD OPEN (0:00-0:30, ~75-100 words)

**Goal**: Hook viewers in first 5 seconds before they swipe away

**Format**:
```
[VISUAL: Compelling graphic or B-roll related to topic]

NARRATION: [Provocative statement or question that creates immediate curiosity]

[VISUAL: Quick cuts showing the problem/phenomenon]

NARRATION: [Promise of the insight - what they'll learn]

[VISUAL: Your face on camera OR animated title card]

NARRATION: [Brief setup of what's coming]
```

**Example**:
```
[VISUAL: Montage of productivity gurus, motivational posters, planners]

NARRATION: Most productivity advice is based on what philosopher David Deutsch calls "bad explanations." And that's not just academic - it's why your productivity system keeps failing.

[VISUAL: Title card: "Why Productivity Advice Fails the Deutsch Test"]

NARRATION: I'm going to show you how to tell the difference between advice that works and advice that just sounds good. And once you see this pattern, you can't unsee it.

[VISUAL: Cut to you on camera]
```

---

### INTRO (0:30-1:30, ~150-225 words)

**Goal**: Establish credibility, set expectations, build rapport

**Format**:
```
[VISUAL: You on camera, well-lit, clear audio]

NARRATION: [Quick intro if needed - who you are, why you're qualified to discuss this]

[VISUAL: Can stay on you OR cut to relevant imagery]

NARRATION: [Establish the problem more thoroughly]

[VISUAL: Examples, screenshots, or graphics illustrating the problem]

NARRATION: [Preview the structure - what you'll cover]

[VISUAL: Back to you]

NARRATION: [Set expectations and invitation to engage]
```

**Example**:
```
[VISUAL: You on camera]

NARRATION: If you're watching this, you've probably tried multiple productivity systems. Morning routines, time blocking, Getting Things Done, maybe Atomic Habits. And they work... for a while. Then they don't.

[VISUAL: Cut to screenshots of productivity apps, book covers, etc.]

NARRATION: The problem isn't you. It's that most productivity advice fails a simple test that distinguishes real knowledge from arbitrary rules.

[VISUAL: Back to you]

NARRATION: Today I'm going to show you that test, apply it to popular productivity advice, and give you a framework that actually works. If this helps you, hit that like button so more people can find it. Let's dive in.
```

### SECTION 1: THE PROBLEM (1:30-4:00, ~400-600 words)

**Goal**: Make viewers feel the problem viscerally

**Format**:
```
[VISUAL: Section title card or graphic]

NARRATION: [State the problem clearly]

[VISUAL: Specific examples, relatable scenarios]

NARRATION: [Tell a story or walk through an example]

[ACTION: Use gestures to emphasize points]

[VISUAL: Graphics, animations, or B-roll illustrating the issue]

NARRATION: [Explain why this matters - consequences]

[VISUAL: Data, charts, or testimonials if available]

NARRATION: [Build to the question: "So what's really going on?"]
```

**Elements to Include**:
- Specific, relatable scenario
- Common experiences viewers have had
- Why existing explanations feel insufficient
- Transition to: "Here's what most people think..."

**Visual Ideas**:
- Screen recordings of productivity apps
- Animations showing concepts
- Text on screen highlighting key points
- Your face reacting/explaining
- B-roll of relevant activities

---

### SECTION 2: COMMON EXPLANATIONS (4:00-7:00, ~500-750 words)

**Goal**: Present and critique popular views fairly

**Format**:
```
[VISUAL: Title: "What People Usually Think"]

NARRATION: [Introduce most common explanation]

[VISUAL: Graphics, quotes, or examples of this view]

NARRATION: [Explain why it seems plausible]

[ACTION: Acknowledge what it gets right]

[VISUAL: "But here's the problem..." graphic]

NARRATION: [Introduce the Deutsch test - "hard to vary"]

[VISUAL: Animation showing how you can swap components]

NARRATION: [Demonstrate that the explanation is arbitrary]

[VISUAL: Back to you for emphasis]

NARRATION: [Repeat for 1-2 more common explanations if time]
```

**Key Teaching Moment**:
This is where you introduce Deutsch's "good explanations" framework:

```
[VISUAL: Split screen - bad explanation on left, variations on right]

NARRATION: David Deutsch, a physicist and philosopher, has a simple test: A good explanation is "hard to vary" while still accounting for what it explains.

[VISUAL: Animation showing parts of explanation being swapped]

NARRATION: Watch what happens with "wake up at 5am to be productive." I can swap that for 6am, or 9am, or "whenever you naturally wake up" - and the advice still seems to "explain" productivity. That means the specific time is arbitrary.

[VISUAL: Contrast with hard-to-vary explanation]

NARRATION: Compare that to: "Match difficult work to your peak cognitive hours." Now try to vary it. If you mismatch work to tired hours, it doesn't work. The components are necessary, not arbitrary. That's a better explanation.
```

**Pacing Tips**:
- Pause after key points (2-3 seconds)
- Use visual punctuation (graphics, cuts)
- Vary your energy - build excitement
- Keep each explanation to 1-2 minutes max

---

### SECTION 3: THE BETTER EXPLANATION (7:00-11:00, ~700-1000 words)

**Goal**: Build the good explanation step-by-step

**Format**:
```
[VISUAL: Section title: "A Better Explanation"]

NARRATION: [State the explanation clearly]

[VISUAL: Animated diagram showing the mechanism]

NARRATION: [Explain HOW it works, not just WHAT it is]

[ACTION: Use hand gestures to show relationships]

[VISUAL: Real-world examples, case studies]

NARRATION: [Show why this is hard to vary]

[VISUAL: Graphics showing what breaks if you change it]

NARRATION: [Demonstrate "reach" - what else it explains]

[VISUAL: Multiple examples showing broader application]

NARRATION: [Address potential objections]

[ACTION: Acknowledge limitations honestly]
```

**Structure This Section As**:

1. **The Core Mechanism** (2 minutes)
   - What actually causes the phenomenon
   - Why these components are necessary
   - Visual representation of how it works

2. **Why It's Hard to Vary** (1 minute)
   - Show what breaks if you change components
   - Demonstrate it's not arbitrary
   - Contrast with easy-to-vary explanations

3. **The Reach** (1.5 minutes)
   - What else this explains
   - Unexpected predictions
   - Broader applications

4. **Evidence** (0.5 minutes)
   - Supporting data or examples
   - What would falsify this
   - How it's been tested

**Visual Ideas**:
- Animated diagrams of the mechanism
- Before/after comparisons
- Case study walk-throughs
- Charts or data visualization
- Screen recordings of applications

## PACING AND ENERGY

### Energy Map

Mark energy levels throughout:

```
[0:00-0:30] HIGH - Hook them fast
[0:30-1:30] MEDIUM-HIGH - Build interest
[1:30-4:00] MEDIUM - Explain problem
[4:00-7:00] HIGH - Teaching moment
[7:00-11:00] MEDIUM-HIGH - Build explanation
[11:00-14:00] HIGH - Practical application
[14:00-16:00] MEDIUM - Broader themes
[16:00-17:00] HIGH - Strong close
```

### Retention Strategies

**Every 90-120 seconds**, include:
- Pattern interrupt (visual change, music change, your movement)
- Callback to hook ("Remember what I said about...")
- Micro-payoff (small insight or revelation)
- Forward promise ("In a moment, I'll show you...")

### Pacing Techniques

- **Vary sentence length**: Mix short punchy statements with longer explanations
- **Pause for emphasis**: 2-3 second pauses after key points
- **Speed up for excitement**: Talk faster when building energy
- **Slow down for importance**: Deliberate pace for key concepts
- **Use silence**: Strategic silence for effect

## STYLE GUIDE

### Narration Style

**Do**:
- Speak conversationally (like talking to a friend)
- Use "you" and "I" freely
- Ask rhetorical questions
- Show enthusiasm for ideas
- Vary your tone and pace
- Use concrete examples
- Tell mini-stories

**Don't**:
- Read like a lecture
- Use overly formal language
- Speak in monotone
- Rush through key points
- Assume too much knowledge
- Use unexplained jargon

### Visual Style

**Consistency**:
- Same title card style throughout
- Consistent color scheme
- Matching graphics style
- Coherent B-roll aesthetic

**Clarity**:
- Text large enough to read on phone
- High contrast for readability
- Clean, uncluttered graphics
- Smooth transitions (not distracting)

NARRATION: That means the specific detail - 5am - is arbitrary. It's not actually explaining what causes productivity.

[VISUAL: Section card: "What To Do Instead"]

NARRATION: So here's what this means for you.

[VISUAL: Numbered list appearing]

[B-ROLL: Person tracking energy levels in notebook]

NARRATION: Two: Categorize your work by what cognitive state it requires. Creative tasks need different energy than administrative tasks.

[VISUAL: Example task categorization]

NARRATION: Three: Match them. Schedule demanding work during your peak cognitive hours. Not 5am because some guru said so - YOUR peak hours.

[VISUAL: Example calendar with matched tasks]

NARRATION: And four: Ruthlessly protect that match. No meetings during deep work time. No notifications during creative time.

[VISUAL: You on camera]

[ACTION: Direct eye contact]

NARRATION: Build YOUR system around the actual mechanism, not someone else's arbitrary schedule.

[4:45 - CONCLUSION]

[VISUAL: You on camera]

NARRATION: Here's the key insight: Good explanations are hard to vary. Bad explanations can be modified to fit anything.

[VISUAL: Quick comparison graphic]

NARRATION: The next time you hear productivity advice - or ANY advice - ask yourself: Could I swap in different details and still have it "work"? If yes, you're probably dealing with a bad explanation.

[ACTION: Lean in slightly]

NARRATION: If this changed how you think about productivity, hit that like button and subscribe for more videos applying David Deutsch's framework to practical problems.

[VISUAL: Subscribe button animation appears]

## REMEMBER

Video is about:
- **Showing**, not just telling
- **Engaging** eyes and ears simultaneously
- **Maintaining** energy and pacing
- **Delivering** clear value
- **Respecting** viewer's time

Every second must earn its place. Cut ruthlessly in editing.

The goal: Viewer leaves thinking differently about the problem and equipped with a practical framework they can actually use.

Would they share this? Would they watch it again? If not, keep editing.
