## COMPLETE EXAMPLE: SHORT-FORM

```
Most "productivity advice" fails the same test.

You know the advice: Wake up at 5am. Do deep work first. Time-block your calendar. Use the Pomodoro technique.

Here's the problem:

These tips are what philosopher David Deutsch calls "easy-to-vary" explanations.

You can swap 5am for 6am, time-blocking for energy management, Pomodoro for Flow states—and the advice still "works" (until it doesn't).

Easy to vary = arbitrary = not explaining what actually drives productivity.

Better explanation:

Productivity comes from matching work type to energy/attention state, then protecting that match from interruption.

This is hard to vary. Change the components → explanation breaks.

And it has "reach"—it explains:
• Why morning routines work for some, not others (different energy patterns)
• Why context-switching kills productivity (breaks the match)
• Why remote work helps some, hurts others (control over matching)

What this means practically:

Don't copy someone else's routine. Instead:
1. Map your energy patterns
2. Match task types to those patterns
3. Ruthlessly protect high-energy time
4. Optimize around YOUR patterns, not "best practices"

Good explanations beat popular advice every time.

What "productivity best practices" have you found don't actually work?

#Productivity #CriticalThinking #WorkSmart
```

## COMPLETE EXAMPLE: LONG-FORM

```
The most expensive mistake in hiring isn't hiring the wrong person.

It's using bad explanations to make hiring decisions.

Let me explain what I mean.

== The Standard Approach ==

Most companies hire based on:
• Resume screening (credentials, experience)
• Interview performance (can they answer questions well?)
• "Culture fit" (do we like them?)
• References (what others say)

This seems reasonable. Everyone does it this way.

But watch what happens when we apply David Deutsch's test for good explanations.

== The Easy-to-Vary Problem ==

Notice: You can swap in different credentials, different interview questions, different definitions of "culture fit"—and the process still "works."

Until you hire someone who fails. Or miss someone who would have been great.

That's because these explanations are "easy to vary"—they can fit any outcome, so they explain nothing.

Bad explanations feel comprehensive, but they're actually arbitrary.

== What Actually Predicts Success ==

Better explanation:

Job success = ability to create knowledge in this domain + incentive alignment with outcomes.

This is hard to vary. You can't replace "create knowledge" with "has experience" (not the same). You can't remove "incentive alignment" without losing explanatory power.

And it has reach—it explains:
• Why experienced hires sometimes fail (couldn't create NEW knowledge for new context)
• Why inexperienced people sometimes excel (high knowledge-creation ability)
• Why "culture fit" is misleading (tests comfort, not alignment)
• Why credentials don't predict performance (credentials ≠ knowledge creation ability)

== What This Changes ==

Instead of asking:
• "Do they have 5 years experience?"

Ask:
• "Can they solve problems they haven't seen before?"
• "How do they learn when they're wrong?"

Instead of asking:
• "Did they go to a top school?"

Ask:
• "Can they create knowledge in this domain?"
• "How do they handle unknown unknowns?"

Instead of asking:
• "Are they a culture fit?"

Ask:
• "Are their incentives aligned with the outcomes we need?"

== Testing This ==

We implemented this at [company/scenario—if applicable, otherwise remove this section]:

Changed hiring process to:
1. Give candidates real problems (not interview questions)
2. Observe knowledge creation process
3. Test error correction (give feedback, see response)
4. Align incentives explicitly

Results: [X improvement] / Or: This framework suggests testing these approaches...

== The Meta-Point ==

This isn't just about hiring.

It's about good vs. bad explanations driving decisions.

Most "best practices" in business are easy to vary—they're bad explanations that persist because:
1. They seem plausible
2. Everyone uses them
3. They can't be easily falsified (confirm whatever happens)

But Deutsch's framework gives us a tool:

Ask: "Can I vary the details while keeping the 'explanatory power'?"

If yes → probably a bad explanation
If no → might be onto something real

== Your Turn ==

What business "best practices" in your domain are actually just bad explanations?

I'm collecting examples of where Deutsch's framework reveals better approaches to common business problems.

What should I analyze next?

#Hiring #Leadership #CriticalThinking #BusinessStrategy #Innovation
```

## LINKEDIN-SPECIFIC GUIDELINES

### Tone
- **Professional but not corporate-speak**
- **Thoughtful but not academic**
- **Helpful but not preachy**
- **Evidence-based but not condescending**

### Voice
- Use "I" when sharing experience
- Use "you" when addressing reader
- Use "we" for shared professional challenges
- Be personal but professional

### Structure
- Short paragraphs (1-3 sentences)
- Liberal use of line breaks
- == Section Headers == (use equals signs)
- Bullet points and lists
- Strategic bolding (sparingly)

### Length Strategy

**Short (<1300 chars)**:
✓ Single clear insight
✓ Quick professional win
✓ High engagement potential
✓ Easy to consume

**Long (1300-3000 chars)**:
✓ Deeper exploration
✓ More substantial value
✓ Builds authority
✓ Requires stronger hook

### Engagement Optimization

**LinkedIn Algorithm Favors**:
- Posts that spark thoughtful comments
- Content that gets shared
- Posts people spend time reading
- Genuine engagement (not just likes)

**Therefore**:
- End with genuine questions
- Invite professional discussion
- Share actionable insights
- Be specific, not generic

## COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

### Don't:
❌ Make it too academic (this isn't a journal)
❌ Use unexplained philosophy jargon
❌ Write like a blog post (different medium)
❌ Be controversial for controversy's sake
❌ Ignore the professional context
❌ End without engagement driver
❌ Overuse hashtags
❌ Write walls of text without breaks

### Do:
✓ Lead with professional insight
✓ Explain framework concepts simply
✓ Use formatting for readability
✓ Be thoughtful and nuanced
✓ Connect to business outcomes
✓ Invite genuine discussion
✓ Use 3-5 relevant hashtags
✓ Break into digestible chunks
