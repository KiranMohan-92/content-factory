# Email Newsletter Template

## Newsletter Philosophy
- Personal and direct (writing to a friend)
- Value-dense (respect their inbox)
- Conversational but substantive
- Build relationship over time

---

## STRUCTURE

### SUBJECT LINE

**Goal**: Get them to open (but don't clickbait)

**Techniques**:
- Curiosity gap: "The problem with [common thing]"
- Direct value: "How good explanations beat best practices"
- Personal: "What I learned about [topic] this week"
- Question: "Why does [phenomenon] happen?"
- Contrarian: "Actually, [contrary to popular belief]"

**Examples**:
- "Why 'follow your passion' is a bad explanation"
- "The framework that changed how I make decisions"
- "Good vs. bad explanations (and why it matters)"
- "What David Deutsch taught me about [practical topic]"

**Length**: 40-50 characters ideal (shows fully on mobile)

---

### PREHEADER TEXT

**What it is**: The preview text after subject line

**Goal**: Support the subject line, add context

**Examples**:
- Subject: "Why 'follow your passion' is a bad explanation"
- Preheader: "And what actually works for career satisfaction"

**Length**: 80-100 characters

## COMPLETE EXAMPLE

---

**Subject**: Why most productivity advice is a bad explanation

**Preheader**: The framework that explains why it doesn't work

---

Hey everyone,

I've been thinking about why so much productivity advice feels both plausible and useless at the same time.

You know the type: "Wake up at 5am." "Do deep work first." "Time-block your calendar."

It sounds good. Successful people say it works for them. But when you try it... meh.

I think I figured out why.

== The Problem with Popular Advice ==

Most productivity advice is what philosopher David Deutsch calls an "easy-to-vary" explanation.

Here's what that means:

Take "wake up at 5am to be productive." Now swap "5am" for "6am" or "whenever you naturally wake up." The advice still "explains" productivity, right?

Or take "time-blocking." Swap it for "energy management" or "getting into flow." Still works as an "explanation."

When you can change the details without breaking the explanation, Deutsch says it's a BAD explanation. The specifics are arbitrary—they're not actually explaining what causes productivity.

Bad explanations feel comprehensive but are actually just word clouds. They can be molded to fit anything, which means they explain nothing.

== Good Explanations Are Different ==

A good explanation is "hard to vary."

Try this one:

"Productivity comes from matching work type to your cognitive state, then protecting that match from interruption."

Now try to vary it:
- Remove "matching" → explanation breaks (why would random tasks work?)
- Remove "protecting from interruption" → explanation breaks (why does context-switching kill productivity?)
- Swap "cognitive state" for "time of day" → loses explanatory power (why do some people work better at night?)

The components are necessary, not arbitrary.

And notice the reach—it explains MORE than just productivity:
- Why remote work helps some people (more control over matching)
- Why open offices hurt productivity (impossible to protect from interruption)
- Why you can't copy someone else's routine (different cognitive patterns)
- Why energy management matters more than time management

That's a good explanation.

== What This Means For You ==

Instead of copying someone's morning routine, ask:

1. What are MY cognitive patterns? (When am I sharp vs. scattered?)
2. What work requires what state? (Creative vs. analytical vs. administrative)
3. How can I match them? (Hard problems during sharp time)
4. How can I protect the match? (No meetings during deep work time)

Build YOUR system around the mechanism, not around someone else's arbitrary details.

This is why "best practices" often fail—they're optimized for someone else's context, not yours.

== The Meta-Point ==

This isn't just about productivity.

It's about recognizing good vs. bad explanations everywhere:
- Career advice
- Business strategy
- Health recommendations
- Relationship guidance

Most popular advice is easy to vary. It persists because it can't be proven wrong—it adapts to any outcome.

But good explanations are risky. They make specific claims. They can be wrong. That's why they're actually useful.

## NEWSLETTER-SPECIFIC GUIDELINES

### Tone
- **Conversational**: Write like you talk
- **Personal**: Use "I" and "you"
- **Intimate**: Like emailing a smart friend
- **Genuine**: Share real thoughts, not just polished content

### Voice
- More casual than articles
- Can be more exploratory
- Can show uncertainty
- Can be more personal

### Length Options

**Quick Hit** (400-600 words):
- One main idea
- Brief explanation
- Quick takeaway
- Fast read

**Standard** (800-1200 words):
- Full explanation
- Examples and application
- Substantial value
- Medium read

**Deep Dive** (1500-2000 words):
- Thorough exploration
- Multiple examples
- Rich implications
- Long read (but earned)

### Frequency Considerations

**Weekly**:
- Sustainable pace
- Can go deeper
- Builds relationship
- Expected by readers

**Bi-weekly**:
- More research time
- Higher quality bar
- Less pressure
- Still regular

**Variable**:
- "When I have something worth saying"
- Higher quality
- Less predictable
- Harder to build habit with readers

### Email Specifics

**From Name**: [Your Name] or [Your Name] - [Newsletter Name]

**Subject Line**: No clickbait, but use curiosity

**Preview Text**: Support the subject line

**Signature**: Consistent sign-off

**Links**:
- Use descriptive text, not naked URLs
- Open in new tab (if possible)
- Don't overlink (distracting)

### What Makes Email Different

**Pros**:
- Direct to inbox (intimate)
- Full attention (no algorithm)
- Replies possible (two-way)
- Ownership of list (not platform-dependent)

**Cons**:
- Competes with other email (high bar)
- Can't edit after sending (must be right first time)
- Unsubscribes happen (don't take personally)
- Slower growth (but higher quality)

## COMMON MISTAKES

### Don't:
❌ Send without proofreading (can't edit after)
❌ Clickbait subject lines (breaks trust)
❌ Include too many links (scattered attention)
❌ Write like a blog post (different medium)
❌ Be inconsistent (erratic schedule hurts engagement)
❌ Forget the preview text (missed opportunity)
❌ Make it all about you (provide value)
❌ Send when you have nothing to say (quality > frequency)

### Do:
✓ Proofread carefully (especially links)
✓ Write subject lines you'd click
✓ Focus on one main idea
✓ Write conversationally
✓ Maintain consistent schedule (even if infrequent)
✓ Optimize preview text
✓ Make it about reader value
✓ Only send when you have something worthwhile

## WRITING PROCESS

### 1. Determine Main Idea
What's the one thing this email is about?

### 2. Draft Subject Line
What would make you open this?

### 3. Write Opening
Personal, warm, preview value

### 4. Write Main Content
Follow one of the structures above

### 5. Add Practical Section
Make it actionable

### 6. Write Closing
Invite engagement

### 7. Edit Ruthlessly
- Cut unnecessary words
- Clarify unclear sections
- Check flow
- Verify links

### 8. Optimize Subject + Preview
- Does subject line work?
- Does preview support it?
- Would you click?

### 9. Proofread
- Grammar and spelling
- Link functionality
- Formatting
- Names and attributions

### 10. Schedule
- Best time for your audience
- Consistent day/time if possible
- Not holidays or weird hours
