# Article Template

## Target Length
2000-3000 words

## Structure

---

### TITLE
[Create a compelling title that hints at the insight without giving it all away]

**Title Options**:
- Question form: "Why Does [Common Belief] Fail to Explain [Phenomenon]?"
- Provocative: "Everything You Know About [Topic] Is Based on a Bad Explanation"
- Promise: "The Real Reason [Phenomenon] Happens (And What It Means)"
- Mystery: "[Paradox] Finally Explained"

---

### HOOK (100-200 words)

[Open with something that immediately captures attention]

**Hook Techniques**:
- Start with a puzzle or paradox
- Open with surprising fact
- Tell a brief story
- Ask a provocative question
- Make a bold claim
- Present a common confusion

**Goal**: Make the reader think "I need to understand this" within the first 30 seconds.

**Example Structure**:
```
[Attention-grabber: surprising fact, question, or claim]

[1-2 sentences establishing why this matters]

[Preview the journey: "The answer reveals something profound about..."]
```

## Style Guidelines

### Voice
- Intelligent but accessible
- Confident but not arrogant
- Clear but not simplistic
- Engaged but not preachy

### Sentences
- Vary length (mix short punchy sentences with longer explanatory ones)
- Prefer active voice
- One main idea per sentence
- Use "you" and "we" judiciously

### Paragraphs
- 2-4 sentences typically
- One main point per paragraph
- White space is your friend
- Break up long sections with subheadings

### Examples
- Use concrete, specific examples
- Make them relatable
- Don't overuse the same examples Deutsch uses
- Mix historical, contemporary, and hypothetical

### Tone
- Respectful of all views (even while criticizing)
- Honest about uncertainty
- Enthusiastic about ideas
- Focused on truth, not scoring points

## What to Avoid

- Don't start with definitions
- Don't use "In this article I will..."
- Don't end with "In conclusion..."
- Don't condescend to reader or other views
- Don't use jargon without explanation
- Don't make claims stronger than evidence supports
- Don't forget examples and concrete details
- Don't make it about you (unless personal story serves the point)

## Formatting

### Subheadings
- Use liberally (every 300-500 words)
- Make them descriptive, not cute
- Use hierarchy (H2, H3) for structure

### Emphasis
- Use **bold** for key terms or important points
- Use *italics* for emphasis or technical terms
- Use sparingly - too much emphasis = no emphasis

### Lists
- Use when presenting multiple points
- Bullets for unordered
- Numbers for sequences or rankings
- Keep items parallel in structure

### Pull Quotes
- Highlight key insights that deserve special attention
- Can be your own words (best phrases from the text)
- Place strategically for visual interest

## Pre-Publication Checklist

Content:
- [ ] Core insight is clear and accurate

- [ ] Bad explanations are criticized fairly
- [ ] Good explanation is genuinely hard to vary
- [ ] Examples are concrete and accurate
- [ ] Implications are practical and insightful

Structure:
- [ ] Hook captures attention immediately
- [ ] Problem is clearly established
- [ ] Flow is logical and smooth
- [ ] Conclusion is satisfying
- [ ] Length is appropriate (2000-3000 words)

Style:
- [ ] Voice is consistent
- [ ] Sentences are clear
- [ ] Paragraphs are digestible
- [ ] Subheadings are helpful
- [ ] No jargon without explanation

Accuracy:
- [ ] Facts are verified
- [ ] Sources are cited

- [ ] Claims are supported

Engagement:
- [ ] Opening is strong
- [ ] Middle maintains interest

- [ ] Ending is memorable

## Example Opening (for reference)

---

**Title**: "Why 'Follow Your Passion' Is a Bad Explanation (And What Actually Works)"

**Hook**:

"Follow your passion" is the most popular career advice of our generation. It's repeated by commencement speakers, self-help gurus, and well-meaning parents everywhere. There's just one problem: it's a terrible explanation of how people end up doing work they love.

Not because it's wrong—many people who love their work are indeed passionate about it. But because the explanation itself is what philosopher David Deutsch would call "easy to vary." And that makes it not just unhelpful, but actively misleading.

Here's why this matters: Bad explanations about career satisfaction lead to bad decisions. They send people on expensive soul-searching quests, create unrealistic expectations, and paradoxically make finding meaningful work harder. A better explanation exists—one that's harder to vary, has clear mechanisms, and actually helps people make better choices.

## Notes for Different Article Types

### Deep Dives
- Can go longer (3000-4000 words)
- More thorough examination of explanations
- More examples and case studies
- Deeper integration with Deutsch's framework

### Practical Applications
- Shorter (2000-2500 words)
- More focus on implications
- More actionable takeaways
- Less theoretical exposition

### Explainers
- Clear problem statement
- Systematic comparison of explanations
- Patient building of good explanation
- Teaching mode throughout

### Critiques
- Fair representation of view being criticized
- Strong evidence for criticism
- Constructive alternative offered
- Respectful tone throughout

Remember: The goal is to help readers understand something better through the power of good explanations. Every word should serve that goal.
