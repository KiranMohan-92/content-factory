# The 5-Test Framework for Good Explanations

**Based on**: David Deutsch's *The Beginning of Infinity* (2011)
**Part of**: Shared Deutsch Framework v1.0
**Last Updated**: 2025-02-05

---

## Overview

Good explanations are the foundation of all knowledge. David Deutsch identifies five independent tests that distinguish good explanations from bad ones. Each test targets a specific failure mode.

---

## The Five Tests

### Test 1: Hard-to-Vary

**Question**: Can I swap components without losing explanatory power?

**What It Detects**: Ad-hoc explanations, arbitrary claims

**Good Explanation**: Components cannot be substituted without breaking the explanation
**Bad Explanation**: Components can be freely swapped and it still "works"

**Example**:
- **Good**: "Earth's 23.5° axial tilt causes seasons"
  - Change tilt to 0° → No seasons (breaks)
  - Change tilt to 90° → Different seasons (breaks)
  - Hard to vary

- **Bad**: "The gods want variety in weather"
  - Change "gods" to "spirits" → Still "explains" seasons
  - Easy to vary

**Scoring**:
- **PASS**: Substitutions break the explanation
- **FAIL**: Substitutions still work

**Why It Matters**: Easy-to-vary explanations are arbitrary. If you can change components without breaking it, the explanation isn't getting at the underlying reality.

---

### Test 2: Mechanism

**Question**: Does it explain HOW (causal mechanism) or just WHAT (description)?

**What It Detects**: "Just-so" stories, descriptive accounts with no causality

**Good Explanation**: Clear causal chain showing how X causes Y
**Bad Explanation**: Describes pattern or correlation without mechanism

**Example**:
- **Good**: "Bacteria reproduce randomly → Some develop resistance → Resistant bacteria survive → Population becomes resistant" (mechanism)
- **Bad**: "Some bacteria are resistant to antibiotics" (description only)

**Scoring**:
- **PASS**: Clear mechanism, causal chain evident
- **FAIL**: Descriptive only, no causal account

**Why It Matters**: Descriptions tell us WHAT happens. Mechanisms tell us WHY—and let us predict new situations and intervene.

---

### Test 3: Reach

**Question**: Does it explain more than originally intended?

**What It Detects**: Narrow explanations that only solve the target problem

**Good Explanation**: Explains multiple phenomena beyond the original domain
**Bad Explanation**: Only explains what it was designed for

**Example**:
- **Good**: Evolution explains biodiversity, fossil records, DNA similarities, antibiotic resistance, vestigial organs, and more (reach)
- **Bad**: "This organism is adapted to its environment" (narrow, just describes one thing)

**Scoring**:
- **PASS**: Has reach - explains multiple phenomena
- **FAIL**: Narrow - only explains target phenomenon

**Why It Matters**: Good explanations have explanatory power beyond their original design. This reach is evidence they connect to reality.

---

### Test 4: Rejectability (Falsifiability)

**Question**: What would show this is wrong? Can we test it?

**What It Detects**: Unfalsifiable claims, immune to criticism

**Good Explanation**: Clear conditions that would prove it wrong
**Bad Explanation**: No way to test or refute

**Example**:
- **Good**: "Drug X cures disease Y" → Test: Give to patients, measure outcomes (testable)
- **Bad**: "Everything happens for a reason" → No test possible (untestable)

**Scoring**:
- **PASS**: Rejectable - clear falsification conditions
- **FAIL**: Unfalsifiable - no way to prove wrong

**Why It Matters**: Unfalsifiable explanations are immune to criticism. They cannot be improved through error elimination because they cannot be tested.

---

### Test 5: Integration

**Question**: Does it connect to other good explanations or conflict?

**What It Detects**: Isolated explanations that stand alone or contradict established knowledge

**Good Explanation**: Connects to and supports other good explanations
**Bad Explanation**: Conflicts with established knowledge or stands alone

**Example**:
- **Good**: Quantum mechanics integrates with chemistry, materials science, and electronics (integrated)
- **Bad**: "Memory water" conflicts with chemistry, physics, and thermodynamics (isolated/conflicting)

**Scoring**:
- **PASS**: Integrated with other knowledge
- **FAIL**: Isolated or conflicts

**Why It Matters**: Knowledge is interconnected. Explanations that contradict established knowledge are likely wrong. Explanations that integrate are more likely to be true.

## Using the 5-Test Framework

### For Evaluating Existing Explanations

**Process**:
1. Identify the explanation's core components
2. Apply all 5 tests systematically
3. Score each test (PASS/FAIL) with evidence
4. Count total passes (X/5)
5. **5/5 = Excellent**, 4/5 = Good, 3/5 = Partial, ≤2/5 = Poor

**Template**:
```
Explanation: [name]

Test 1 - Hard-to-Vary: [PASS/FAIL]
Evidence: [substitution attempts and results]

Test 2 - Mechanism: [PASS/FAIL]
Evidence: [causal chain OR "no mechanism found"]

Test 3 - Reach: [PASS/FAIL]
Evidence: [what else it explains OR "only explains target"]

Test 4 - Rejectability: [PASS/FAIL]
Evidence: [falsification conditions OR "not falsifiable"]

Test 5 - Integration: [PASS/FAIL]
Evidence: [connections to other knowledge OR "isolated/conflicts"]

Overall Score: X/5
Status: [Excellent/Good/Partial/Poor]
```

### For Creating New Explanations

**Design Principle**: Build explanations that pass all 5 tests from the start

**Process**:
1. Start with the problem
2. Conjecture mechanisms (not just patterns)
3. Ask: "What would falsify this?"
4. Ask: "What ELSE would this explain?"
5. Ask: "How does this connect to what we know?"
6. Test for hard-to-vary: Can I vary components?

## Common Failure Modes

| Test | Common Failure | How to Avoid |
|------|----------------|---------------|
| Hard-to-Vary | "X causes Y" with arbitrary X | Make mechanism necessary, not optional |
| Mechanism | Describing patterns, not causes | Always include causal chain |
| Reach | Narrow, single-domain explanations | Look for broader implications |
| Rejectability | Unfalsifiable claims | Always specify falsification conditions |
| Integration | Contradicting established knowledge | Check for conflicts with good explanations |

## Relationship to Other Framework Concepts

### Connection to Conjecture & Criticism

The 5-test framework is the **criticism** component of conjecture-and-criticism. It provides systematic ways to criticize conjectures.

### Connection to Reach

Reach (Test 3) is a special test of explanatory power. Good explanations have reach because they're describing deep truths about reality.

### Connection to Problem-Solving

Problems are soluble through better explanations. The 5-test framework helps us identify which explanations are good enough to solve problems.

## Examples in Practice

### Science

**Evolution by Natural Selection**:
- Hard-to-Vary: ✓ (Can't vary without breaking)
- Mechanism: ✓ (Variation + selection = adaptation)
- Reach: ✓ (Explains all of biology)
- Rejectability: ✓ (Can falsify with predictions)
- Integration: ✓ (Connects to genetics, paleontology)
- **Score: 5/5 - Good explanation**

### Pseudoscience

**Astrology**:
- Hard-to-Vary: ✗ (Can vary zodiac signs arbitrarily)
- Mechanism: ✗ (No causal mechanism for star position affecting personality)
- Reach: ✗ (Only explains what it was designed for)
- Rejectability: ✗ (Vague predictions, not clearly testable)
- Integration: ✗ (Conflicts with physics)
- **Score: 0/5 - Bad explanation**

## Advanced: Why These Five Tests?

The five tests are **not arbitrary**. Each corresponds to a distinct way explanations can fail:

| Test | Failure Mode Detected | Independent From |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Hard-to-Vary | Ad-hoc, arbitrary | All other tests |
| Mechanism | "Just-so" stories | Reach (without mechanism, reach is superficial) |
| Reach | Narrow scope | Integration (without reach, can't judge integration) |
| Rejectability | Unfalsifiable, immune to criticism | Error elimination (without rejectability, can't improve) |
| Integration | Isolation, contradiction | All other tests (integration without mechanism = connection to bad explanations) |

This is why all 5 are necessary. Each catches errors the others miss.
